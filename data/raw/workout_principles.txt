WORKOUT PRINCIPLES KNOWLEDGE BASE

BEGINNER WORKOUT PRINCIPLES
Beginners should train 3 days per week with full body workouts.
Full body training allows beginners to practice movements more frequently.
Focus on learning correct form before adding weight.
Linear progression works best for beginners — add weight every session.
Beginners can gain strength and muscle simultaneously (newbie gains).
Recommended beginner program structure:
Day 1: Full body workout A
Day 2: Rest
Day 3: Full body workout B
Day 4: Rest
Day 5: Full body workout A
Day 6: Rest
Day 7: Rest

INTERMEDIATE WORKOUT PRINCIPLES
Intermediate lifters benefit from 4 day training splits.
Push Pull Legs is the most popular and effective intermediate split.
Push day: chest, shoulders, triceps
Pull day: back, biceps
Leg day: quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves
Upper Lower split is another effective option for intermediates.
Weekly progression models work better than session to session for intermediates.

ADVANCED WORKOUT PRINCIPLES
Advanced lifters need higher volume and more sophisticated programming.
5-6 day training splits allow more volume per muscle group.
Advanced techniques: drop sets, supersets, rest-pause, mechanical drop sets.
Periodization becomes essential — alternating hypertrophy and strength phases.
Deload weeks every 4-6 weeks are mandatory for advanced lifters.

EXERCISE SELECTION
Compound movements should form the foundation of any program.
Best compound movements: squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, row, pull-up.
Isolation exercises complement compound movements but are not primary.
Exercise order: compound movements first, isolation exercises last.
Weak points should be addressed with targeted isolation work.

SETS AND REPS GUIDELINES
For strength: 3-5 sets of 1-5 reps at 85-95% 1RM
For hypertrophy: 3-5 sets of 8-15 reps at 65-80% 1RM
For endurance: 2-3 sets of 15-30 reps at 50-65% 1RM
For beginners: 3 sets of 8-12 reps is a great starting point
Total weekly sets per muscle group: 10-20 sets for growth

REST PERIODS
Heavy compound lifts: 3-5 minutes rest
Moderate hypertrophy work: 1.5-3 minutes rest
Light isolation work: 45-90 seconds rest
Circuit training: 30-60 seconds rest

CARDIO GUIDELINES
LISS (Low Intensity Steady State): 30-60 min at 60-70% max heart rate
HIIT (High Intensity Interval Training): 15-25 min, 20 sec on 40 sec off
LISS is better for fat loss without impacting muscle recovery
HIIT improves cardiovascular fitness faster but is more taxing
For fat loss: 3-5 cardio sessions per week
For muscle gain: limit cardio to 2-3 sessions to avoid interfering with recovery
Best cardio for muscle preservation: walking, cycling, swimming

WARM UP AND COOL DOWN
Always warm up before training to prevent injury.
General warm up: 5-10 minutes light cardio to raise heart temperature.
Specific warm up: 2-3 sets with light weight of the first exercise.
Cool down: 5-10 minutes light stretching of worked muscles.
Static stretching is best done after training, not before.
Dynamic stretching before training improves performance and reduces injury risk.
Foam rolling before and after training reduces muscle soreness.

FAT LOSS SPECIFIC TRAINING
Resistance training is more effective than cardio for long term fat loss.
Muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue.
Circuit training combines strength and cardio for time-efficient fat loss.
High rep training does not tone muscles — progressive overload does.
Spot reduction of fat is a myth — fat loss is systemic.
NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) accounts for 15-30% of daily calorie burn.
Increasing daily steps to 8000-10000 significantly accelerates fat loss.
