NUTRITION GUIDE KNOWLEDGE BASE

CALORIE CALCULATION
The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is the most accurate for calculating calorie needs.
For men: BMR = (10 x weight kg) + (6.25 x height cm) - (5 x age) + 5
For women: BMR = (10 x weight kg) + (6.25 x height cm) - (5 x age) - 161
Sedentary lifestyle multiplier: BMR x 1.2
Lightly active multiplier: BMR x 1.375
Moderately active multiplier: BMR x 1.55
Very active multiplier: BMR x 1.725
Athlete multiplier: BMR x 1.9

MACRONUTRIENTS
Protein provides 4 calories per gram and is essential for muscle repair and growth.
Carbohydrates provide 4 calories per gram and are the primary fuel for exercise.
Fats provide 9 calories per gram and are essential for hormone production.

PROTEIN REQUIREMENTS
For fat loss: 2.0-2.4g protein per kg bodyweight to preserve muscle.
For muscle gain: 1.6-2.2g protein per kg bodyweight for maximum synthesis.
For strength: 1.6-2.0g protein per kg bodyweight.
For sedentary individuals: 0.8g protein per kg bodyweight minimum.
Best protein sources: chicken breast, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese,
fish (salmon, tuna), lean beef, legumes, tofu, whey protein.
Distribute protein evenly across 4-5 meals for optimal muscle protein synthesis.
Each meal should contain at least 20-40g of protein to maximize synthesis.

CARBOHYDRATE GUIDELINES
Carbohydrates should make up 40-60% of total calories for active individuals.
Complex carbs are preferred: oats, brown rice, sweet potato, quinoa, whole wheat.
Simple carbs are best consumed around workouts for quick energy.
Pre-workout meal should contain carbs 1-2 hours before training.
Post-workout carbs replenish glycogen stores and aid recovery.
Low carb diets can work for fat loss but may impair high intensity performance.

FAT GUIDELINES
Dietary fats should make up 20-35% of total daily calories.
Essential fatty acids omega-3 and omega-6 must come from diet.
Best fat sources: avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, eggs.
Omega-3 fatty acids reduce inflammation and support recovery.
Avoid trans fats found in processed foods and fried foods.
Saturated fats should be limited to less than 10% of total calories.

MEAL TIMING
Pre-workout meal: 1-2 hours before training, focus on carbs and protein.
Post-workout meal: within 1-2 hours after training, protein and carbs.
The anabolic window is larger than previously thought — up to 24 hours.
Eating protein before bed (casein) supports overnight muscle recovery.
Intermittent fasting can work for fat loss but does not suit everyone.
Meal frequency of 3-5 meals per day works well for most people.

HYDRATION
Water is essential for every metabolic process in the body.
Recommended daily water intake: 35ml per kg of bodyweight.
During exercise drink 500-750ml of water per hour of training.
Dehydration of just 2% reduces performance by up to 20%.
Signs of dehydration: dark urine, fatigue, headache, decreased performance.
Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) are lost through sweat.
Sports drinks are only necessary for exercise lasting more than 60 minutes.

SUPPLEMENTS
Creatine monohydrate is the most researched supplement for strength and muscle.
Creatine dose: 3-5g per day, no loading phase necessary.
Whey protein is convenient but whole food protein is equally effective.
Caffeine improves performance by 3-12% at doses of 3-6mg per kg bodyweight.
Vitamin D deficiency is common and affects muscle function and recovery.
Fish oil supplements provide omega-3 fatty acids for recovery and health.
Most other supplements have weak evidence and are not necessary.
