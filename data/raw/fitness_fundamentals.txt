FITNESS FUNDAMENTALS KNOWLEDGE BASE

PROGRESSIVE OVERLOAD
Progressive overload is the most important principle in fitness training.
It means gradually increasing the stress placed on the body during exercise.
You can progress by increasing weight, reps, sets, or decreasing rest time.
Without progressive overload, your body adapts and stops making progress.
Research shows that progressive overload leads to 40% more muscle growth
compared to training with the same weights every week.

FAT LOSS PRINCIPLES
Fat loss occurs when you consume fewer calories than you burn (caloric deficit).
A deficit of 300-500 calories per day leads to 0.3-0.5 kg of fat loss per week.
This is considered the optimal rate for preserving muscle while losing fat.
Extreme deficits above 1000 calories per day cause muscle loss and metabolic adaptation.
Strength training during fat loss preserves up to 100% of muscle mass.
Cardio accelerates fat loss but should not replace strength training.
High protein intake (1.6-2.2g per kg bodyweight) prevents muscle loss during cutting.

MUSCLE GAIN PRINCIPLES
Muscle hypertrophy requires a caloric surplus of 200-400 calories above maintenance.
Protein synthesis is maximized with 1.6-2.2g of protein per kg of bodyweight daily.
Training volume of 10-20 sets per muscle group per week is optimal for hypertrophy.
Rep ranges of 6-30 reps all build muscle when taken close to failure.
The most effective rep range for hypertrophy is 8-15 reps per set.
Rest periods of 2-3 minutes between sets maximize strength and muscle gains.
Compound movements like squats, deadlifts, bench press build the most muscle.
Natural muscle gain is 0.5-1 kg per month for beginners, 0.25-0.5 kg for intermediates.

STRENGTH BUILDING PRINCIPLES
Strength is best built in the 1-5 rep range with 85-95% of your max weight.
Neural adaptations drive early strength gains before visible muscle growth.
The big four lifts for strength: squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press.
Strength training requires longer rest periods of 3-5 minutes between heavy sets.
Linear progression works best for beginners — adding weight every session.
Intermediate lifters benefit from weekly progression models.
Deload weeks every 4-8 weeks prevent overtraining and improve long term progress.

RECOVERY AND REST
Muscles grow during rest, not during training.
Sleep of 7-9 hours per night is essential for muscle recovery and hormone production.
Growth hormone is primarily released during deep sleep.
Overtraining syndrome occurs when training exceeds recovery capacity.
Signs of overtraining: persistent fatigue, decreased performance, mood changes.
Active recovery (light walking, stretching) on rest days improves blood flow.
Most muscle groups need 48-72 hours of recovery between training sessions.

BMI AND BODY COMPOSITION
BMI (Body Mass Index) is calculated as weight(kg) divided by height(m) squared.
BMI under 18.5 = Underweight
BMI 18.5 to 24.9 = Normal weight
BMI 25 to 29.9 = Overweight
BMI 30 and above = Obese
BMI does not account for muscle mass — athletes often have high BMI but low body fat.
Body fat percentage is a more accurate measure of fitness than BMI.
Healthy body fat for men: 10-20%, for women: 18-28%.
